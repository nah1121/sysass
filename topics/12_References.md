## 1. Primary References (Cited Across All Topics)

1. **Moir, I. & Seabridge, A.** — *Aircraft Systems: Mechanical, Electrical and Avionics Subsystems Integration* (3rd ed.). Wiley-AIAA, 2008. ISBN 978-0-470-05996-8.
   > The definitive integrated reference on all major aircraft systems. Chapters on hydraulic power, flight controls, and electrical systems are directly relevant. Cross-disciplinary treatment is ideal for understanding system integration.

2. **SAE ARP4754A** — *Guidelines for Development of Civil Aircraft and Systems*. SAE International, 2010. ISBN 978-0-7680-7540-6.
   > The master standard for civil aircraft system development. Defines safety assessment process, design assurance levels, and the V-model lifecycle for all aircraft systems including hydraulics.

3. **RTCA DO-178C** — *Software Considerations in Airborne Systems and Equipment Certification*. RTCA, 2011.
   > The standard for certifying safety-critical aviation software. All software in hydraulic ECUs, flight control computers, and landing gear control units must be developed to this standard.

4. **RTCA DO-160G** — *Environmental Conditions and Test Procedures for Airborne Equipment*. RTCA, 2010.
   > Defines qualification tests for all aircraft-installed electronic and electrical equipment including sensors, ECUs, solenoid valves, and wiring.

5. **FAA** — *Aviation Maintenance Technician Handbook — Airframe, Vol. 2*. FAA-H-8083-31A. U.S. Department of Transportation, Federal Aviation Administration.
   > Chapter 12 covers aircraft hydraulic systems; Chapter 11 covers aircraft electrical systems. Essential reference for AMT licensing preparation.

6. **Pallett, E.H.J.** — *Aircraft Electrical Systems* (3rd ed.). Longman, 1987. ISBN 978-0-582-98728-0.
   > Classic reference covering all aspects of aircraft electrical systems including power generation, distribution, and control wiring. Strong on the principles underpinning modern systems.

## 3. Regulatory Documents — FAA

17. **14 CFR Part 25** — *Airworthiness Standards: Transport Category Airplanes*. Federal Aviation Administration.
    > The regulatory basis for all civil transport aircraft certification in the USA.

18. **FAA Advisory Circular AC 25.1309-1A** — *System Design and Analysis*. FAA, 1988.
    > Provides guidance on acceptable means of compliance with §25.1309, including FMEA, FTA, and probability methods.

19. **FAA Advisory Circular AC 25.629-1B** — *Flutter Substantiation Tests for Transport Category Airplanes*. FAA.
    > Relevant to active flutter suppression systems driven by electrohydraulic actuators.

20. **FAA Order 8110.49A** — *Software Approval Guidelines*. FAA, 2011.
    > FAA guidance on implementation of DO-178C for software certification.

21. **FAA-H-8083-31A** — *Aviation Maintenance Technician Handbook — Airframe, Volume 2*. FAA, 2012.
    > Chapters 11 and 12 cover electrical systems and hydraulic systems respectively.

22. **FAA-H-8083-32** — *Aviation Maintenance Technician Handbook — Powerplant*. FAA.
    > Chapter 11 covers auxiliary power, relevant to APU driving hydraulic backup pumps.

## 4. Regulatory Documents — EASA

23. **EASA CS-25** — *Certification Specifications and Acceptable Means of Compliance for Large Aeroplanes*. European Union Aviation Safety Agency.
    > European equivalent of FAR Part 25; directly applicable to Airbus aircraft and other European designs.

24. **EASA AMC 25.1309** — *Assessment of Systems and Equipment for Large Aeroplanes*. EASA.
    > Acceptable means of compliance with CS-25.1309; describes safety assessment methodology in detail.

25. **EASA AMC-20-115D** — *Airborne Software Development Assurance Using EUROCAE ED-12 and RTCA DO-178*. EASA.
    > Formal acceptance of DO-178C as means of compliance for software certification in Europe.

26. **EASA AMC-20-152A** — *Development Assurance of Airborne Electronic Hardware*. EASA.
    > Formal acceptance of DO-254 in Europe.

27. **EASA Part M** — *Continuing Airworthiness Requirements*. EASA.
    > Requirements for continuing airworthiness maintenance of aircraft, including hydraulic electrical systems.

28. **EASA Part 145** — *Requirements for Maintenance Organisations*. EASA.
    > Certification requirements for organisations performing aircraft maintenance (relevant to hydraulic system repair).

29. **EASA AI Roadmap 2.0** — *A Human-Centric Approach to AI in Aviation*. EASA, 2023.
    > Outlines EASA approach to certifying AI/ML in aviation systems, including predictive maintenance applications.

## 5. Industry Standards — SAE, RTCA, MIL

30. **SAE ARP4761** — *Guidelines and Methods for Conducting the Safety Assessment Process on Civil Airborne Systems and Equipment*. SAE International, 1996.
    > Companion to ARP4754A; describes FMEA, FTA, and Common Cause Analysis methods in detail.

31. **RTCA DO-254** — *Design Assurance Guidance for Airborne Electronic Hardware*. RTCA, 2000.
    > Standard for certification of complex airborne electronic hardware (FPGAs, ASICs) used in hydraulic ECUs.

32. **RTCA DO-326A** — *Airworthiness Security Process Specification*. RTCA, 2014.
    > New standard addressing cyber security in aircraft systems, including connected hydraulic monitoring data.

33. **SAE AS5440** — *Hydraulic Actuators: Aircraft, Selection and Application of*. SAE International.
    > Guidance on selecting and applying hydraulic actuators in aircraft design.

34. **MIL-DTL-38999** — *Connectors, Electrical, Circular, Miniature, High Density, Environment Resistant, Scoop-Proof*. U.S. Department of Defense.
    > Military specification for the circular connectors used throughout aircraft hydraulic wiring systems.

35. **MIL-PRF-17693** — *Linear Variable Differential Transformers (LVDTs), General Specification for*. U.S. Department of Defense.
    > Performance specification for aerospace LVDTs.

36. **MIL-PRF-5606 / MIL-PRF-83282** — *Hydraulic Fluid, Petroleum Base / Fire Resistant*. U.S. DoD.
    > Military specifications for hydraulic fluids used in aircraft.

37. **IEC 60751** — *Industrial Platinum Resistance Thermometers and Platinum Temperature Sensors*. IEC, 2008.
    > International standard for RTD (Resistance Temperature Detector) sensors — used in hydraulic fluid temperature measurement.

## 7. Textbooks — Control Systems and Sensors

44. **Ogata, K.** — *Modern Control Engineering* (5th ed.). Prentice Hall, 2010. ISBN 978-0-13-615673-4.
    > Standard textbook on control system theory; covers closed-loop control, stability, Bode plots, root locus — all relevant to electrohydraulic servo systems.

45. **Merritt, H.E.** — *Hydraulic Control Systems*. Wiley, 1967.
    > The classic reference on electrohydraulic servo systems; covers servo valve design, hydraulic actuator dynamics, and loop stability.

46. **Fraden, J.** — *Handbook of Modern Sensors: Physics, Designs, and Applications* (5th ed.). Springer, 2016. ISBN 978-3-319-19302-1.
    > Comprehensive reference on all sensor types including pressure, temperature, position, and flow sensors.

47. **Doebelin, E.O.** — *Measurement Systems: Application and Design* (5th ed.). McGraw-Hill, 2004. ISBN 978-0-07-366308-3.
    > Covers measurement system fundamentals including LVDT, pressure transducer, and thermocouple operation in detail.

48. **Herceg, E.E.** — *Handbook of Measurement and Control*. Schaevitz Engineering, 1976.
    > Classic LVDT reference; describes theory of operation, signal conditioning, and application in aircraft systems.

## 9. Accident Reports and Investigation Documents

52. **NTSB Report AAR-90/06** — *United Airlines Flight 232, McDonnell Douglas DC-10-10, Sioux Gateway Airport, Sioux City, Iowa, July 19, 1989*. National Transportation Safety Board, 1990.
    > Definitive report on total hydraulic system loss; led to strengthened CCA requirements.

53. **NTSB Report AAR-04/04** — *American Airlines Flight 587, Airbus Industrie A300-605R, Belle Harbor, New York, November 12, 2001*. NTSB, 2004.
    > Report on rudder overload accident; relevant to FBW envelope protection discussion.

54. **Austrian Board of Accident Investigation** — *Final Report on the Accident of Lauda Air Luftfahrt AG, Boeing 767-300ER, OE-LAV, near Phu Krathing, Thailand, May 26, 1991*. 1993.
    > Thrust reverser deployment due to solenoid valve failure; led to thrust reverser fail-safe redesign requirements.

55. **BFU Germany** — *Accident Investigation Report AX001-1-2/02 — Germanwings Flight 9525*. Bundesstelle für Flugunfalluntersuchung, 2016.
    > While primarily a human factors/security incident, relevant to autopilot-driven hydraulic actuator commands.

56. **Joint Authorities Technical Review (JATR)** — *Boeing 737 MAX Flight Control System — Observations, Findings, and Recommendations to the FAA*. U.S. Department of Transportation, 2019.
    > Critical review of MCAS design and certification; most important recent document on automated hydraulic control system safety.

57. **NTSB DCA01MA055** — *Air Transat Flight 236, Fuel Exhaustion and Total Engine Failure, Boeing 767-300, August 24, 2001*. NTSB, 2003.
    > Demonstrates RAT hydraulic and electrical backup in real emergency.

58. **BEA France** — *Final Report — Air France Flight 447, Airbus A330-203, Atlantic Ocean, June 1, 2009*. BEA, 2012.
    > While primarily a pitot-static failure, demonstrates how sensor failure cascades through FBW into flight control authority issues.

## Notes on Source Reliability

All references in this series are from:
- **Primary regulatory documents** (FAA, EASA) — authoritative
- **Established technical standards** (SAE, RTCA) — authoritative
- **Peer-reviewed academic textbooks** (Wiley-AIAA, Springer, CRC Press) — authoritative
- **Official manufacturer documentation** (Airbus, Boeing, Parker) — authoritative for their own systems
- **Official accident investigation reports** (NTSB, BEA, AAIB) — authoritative

Where specific numerical values are given (pressure limits, temperatures, response times), these are representative of general industry practice. For specific maintenance actions, **always use the aircraft-specific Approved Maintenance Manual (AMM)** — these documents are the only approved source for maintenance data.
